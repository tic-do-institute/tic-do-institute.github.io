About the Institute

TIC-DO Institute is an independent research organization dedicated to the computational modeling of biological systems. We investigate the mechanisms of maladaptive stability and phase transitions in the human sensorimotor system through the lens of the Free Energy Principle and Active Inference.

Our mission is to bridge the gap between theoretical biophysics and clinical phenomenology. Specifically, we focus on elucidating how informational dynamics influence somatic regulation, identifying the computational failures that lead to "frozen" metastable attractors in physiological states, and developing theoretical frameworks for their resolution.

Research Projects

Under Review Theoretical Framework: Bistable Precision Dynamics and Defensive Functional Decoupling Principal Investigator: Takafumi Shiga

This project establishes the computational foundation of chronic functional disorders. By integrating Active Inference with a Kuramoto model of coupled oscillators, we demonstrate how "Defensive Functional Decoupling" emerges as a metastable attractor driven by precision collapse. The study theoretically proves that recovery requires "Specific Informational Perturbation (SIP)"—a transient salience spike—rather than mechanical forcing, resolving the thermodynamic paradox of functional immobility.

Ongoing / Data Collection Empirical Validation: Randomized Controlled Trial on Proprioceptive Prediction Error Neglect Principal Investigator: Takafumi Shiga (N=106)

A large-scale empirical study (RCT and crossover design) aimed at validating the theoretical predictions of the Defensive Functional Decoupling model. We are currently analyzing kinematic data to identify the statistical signatures of critical slowing down (CSD) and to evaluate the efficacy of informational perturbation in human subjects. This project serves as the experimental counterpart to our theoretical computational models.
